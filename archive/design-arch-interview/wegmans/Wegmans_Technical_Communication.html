Behavioral Communication Retail Context

"We're a grocery company, not a tech company. How do you explain something like medallion architecture or Unity Catalog governance to our VP of Merchandising and head of Supply Chain?"

Wegmans Solutions Architect Prep  ·  Steve Lysik  ·  ~3.5 min answer
Pocket Answer 30–45 sec
"The number one rule I follow is: never lead with the technology — lead with the business problem it solves. Nobody in Merchandising needs to know the word 'medallion.' What they need to hear is: 'Right now, your sales data goes through three stages — raw receipts come in, we clean and match them to products and stores, and then you get a ready-to-use report that shows what's selling, where, and why.' That's the medallion architecture — but I'd never call it that in the room. For governance, I'd say: 'We built one single catalog so every department sees the same version of truth — Merchandising sees product performance, Supply Chain sees inventory velocity, and Finance sees margin — all from the same trusted data, with each team seeing only what's relevant to them.' At Microsoft, I pitched Databricks to C-suite executives across Fortune 500 accounts — the ones who signed off weren't the engineers, they were the business leaders. I learned that if you can't explain it in grocery terms, you don't understand it well enough yourself."
This is the quick version. Scroll down for the full scripted answer with analogy mapping, scenario walk-throughs, and follow-up Q&A.

Why They're Asking This

This is a culture fit test disguised as a communication question. Wegmans is telling you who's in the room: business leaders, not engineers. They've probably had past experiences with technical hires who talked over people's heads and lost the room. They want to know: will you be a bridge between the platform team and the business — or will you be another technologist who alienates non-technical colleagues? The subtext is also: "Our people are smart. Don't condescend to them. Translate for them."

Four Principles of Technical Translation
1
Lead with the Business Problem
Never open with the technology name. Start with the pain they feel today: "You're making decisions off stale spreadsheets" or "Three teams have three different sales numbers."
2
Use Their Vocabulary
Supply Chain says "fill rate" and "lead time." Merchandising says "category performance" and "promotional lift." Map every concept to words they already use daily.
3
Anchor to Grocery Analogies
The best analogies come from the business itself. A medallion architecture is like produce going from farm → distribution center → shelf display. They already live this process.
4
Show the Outcome, Not the Plumbing
The VP of Merchandising doesn't need to know about Delta tables. They need to know: "You'll see yesterday's sales by 7 AM instead of waiting until Thursday for a report."

Full Scripted Answer
Part 1 — Establish the Philosophy (30 sec)
"Great question, and I actually think this is the most important skill a Solutions Architect brings. The technology is only valuable if the business understands what it's doing for them and trusts it. My rule is simple: never lead with the technology name — lead with the business problem it solves. If I'm in a room with your VP of Merchandising, I'm not going to say 'medallion architecture.' I'm going to talk about what they care about — getting better data to make better decisions faster."
Experience Bridge

At Microsoft as a Solutions Architect, I presented Databricks to C-suite executives at Fortune 500 companies. The people who signed the purchase orders weren't the engineers — they were the CFOs and the VPs of Operations. I learned very quickly that if you lose the business leader, you lose the deal, no matter how good the tech is.

Part 2 — Medallion in Grocery Terms (45 sec)
"So let me give you an example. If I'm explaining our data architecture to the head of Supply Chain, I'd say something like: 'Think about what happens when a truck pulls up to the Wegmans distribution center. Everything arrives in different formats — different suppliers, different invoices, different packaging. That's our first stage. Then your team sorts it, checks quality, labels it, and routes it to the right store and the right department. That's the second stage. And finally, products hit the shelf display — curated, organized, ready for the customer to grab and go. That's exactly what we're doing with data. Raw data comes in messy, we clean and organize it, and your team gets a polished report they can act on immediately.' I never used the word 'medallion' — but I just explained the entire architecture."
Part 3 — Unity Catalog Governance (45 sec)
"For governance, I'd use an analogy that everyone at a grocery company already understands: the master product catalog. Every item Wegmans sells has one SKU, one name, one set of attributes. The buyer sees cost and margin. The store manager sees retail price and shelf position. The customer sees the price tag. Same product, different views based on who's looking. That's exactly what Unity Catalog does for data. We have one single source of truth. Merchandising sees product performance. Supply Chain sees inventory velocity. Finance sees margin analysis. Same underlying data, but each team sees exactly what's relevant to their role — and sensitive data like individual customer details stays behind a curtain for people who don't need it. When I frame it that way, the VP doesn't just understand governance — they become an advocate for it, because they can see it protects their data while making their team faster."
Part 4 — The Two-Audience Approach (30 sec)
"I also want to acknowledge something: I adjust my communication based on what each stakeholder actually cares about. The VP of Merchandising? They want to know: 'Will I see promotional ROI faster? Can I compare category performance across stores in real time?' The head of Supply Chain wants to know: 'Will I get demand signals earlier? Can we reduce waste on perishables?' I answer both of those with the same platform — but I frame it in their KPIs, their vocabulary, their daily pain points. The minute I start talking about Delta tables and partition pruning, I've lost the room."
Experience Bridge

At IBM as a Data Solutions Architect, I worked with clients across healthcare, manufacturing, and financial services. Every single one had the same challenge: brilliant technical teams who couldn't get executive buy-in because they were presenting architecture diagrams instead of business outcomes. I made it my role to be the translator in the room. And at Hopewell, I was the Director of Data — meaning I sat in both chairs. I was building the pipelines AND presenting to the board. That dual perspective is something I bring to every conversation.

Part 5 — Wegmans Culture Tie-in (30 sec)
"And I want to add one thing specific to Wegmans. You're a company where people have been here for decades — your department managers, your supply chain leads, your merchandising team. They have incredibly deep domain knowledge. My job isn't to come in and tell them what data means. My job is to give them better tools so their expertise reaches further. When I explain a concept, I'm not dumbing it down — I'm meeting smart people where they are and connecting what we're building to what they already know. That's how you get real adoption — not compliance, but genuine buy-in."
Live Scenario Walk-Throughs

Two real-world scenarios showing the wrong way and right way to explain the same concept.

VP of Merchandising — Category Review Meeting
Explaining: Why data might look different than their old reports
Wrong Approach

"We re-ingested the source tables into Bronze and re-ran the Silver transformations with updated dedup logic and SCD Type 2 merge, so some historical category totals shifted by 2-3% from the legacy Teradata output."

Right Approach

"You may notice some of last quarter's category numbers shifted slightly. That's because we found duplicate transactions in the old system that were inflating totals. We cleaned that up, so what you're seeing now is actually more accurate. Bottom line: your category rankings didn't change, but the numbers are now trustworthy enough to build forecasts on."

Head of Supply Chain — Demand Planning Session
Explaining: Why they should trust the new demand forecast
Wrong Approach

"The model uses an XGBoost ensemble trained on 18 months of Silver-layer POS data joined with Feature Store tables for weather, holidays, and promotional flags, validated against a 20% holdout with a MAPE of 4.2%."

Right Approach

"The new forecast looks at the same things your best planners look at — last year's sales, upcoming holidays, weather patterns, and what promotions are running — but it does it for every SKU in every store, every day. In testing, it was within 4% of actual demand. That should mean fewer empty shelves on the things that matter and less waste on perishables."

Coaching Notes

Do This

Use a real analogy in your answer — the grocery flow one is gold. Show them you can actually do it, not just talk about doing it.
Reference specific Wegmans stakeholders by title: "If I'm with your VP of Merchandising..." makes it tangible.
Mention that you've been on both sides — building AND presenting. The Hopewell board experience is powerful here.
Show respect for tenure and institutional knowledge. Wegmans people have been there for years. Acknowledge that.
End with "adoption, not compliance" — that's a phrase that will resonate with a people-first company.

Avoid This

Don't just SAY "I use simple language" — SHOW IT by actually translating a concept in real time during your answer.
Don't be condescending. "I dumb it down" is the wrong framing. It's "I translate" or "I meet people where they are."
Don't use the word "stakeholder" more than once. It's corporate-speak. Say "your Supply Chain team" or "the Merchandising VP."
Don't forget the governance piece. They asked about Unity Catalog specifically — the product catalog analogy is your strongest play.
Don't rush. This is a question where pacing and tone matter as much as content. Slow down for the analogy parts.
Likely Follow-Up Questions
"What if they push back and say the old reports were fine?"
▾
"That's a completely valid reaction, and honestly, it tells me the old reports were serving a real need. I'd never dismiss that. What I'd say is: 'The old reports got you here — and that's important. What we're doing isn't replacing them, it's upgrading the engine underneath. You'll see the same numbers you trust, but they'll be fresher, they'll be consistent across departments, and when you have a new question — like how a promotion performed by store — you won't have to wait two weeks for IT to build a custom query.' I position it as evolution, not revolution."
"How do you handle a room where some people get it and some don't?"
▾
"I read the room in real time. I watch body language — if someone leans back and crosses their arms, I've lost them. If someone starts nodding, I know the analogy landed. In practice, I layer my explanation: start with the business outcome for the executives, then offer a 'one level deeper' for anyone who wants the technical detail. Something like: 'For anyone who's curious about the how — this uses Delta Live Tables under the hood, which is basically an automated quality-check pipeline. Happy to go deeper after the meeting.' That way the technical people feel respected, and the business people aren't dragged into the weeds."
"How do you get business stakeholders to actually use new dashboards instead of their old Excel files?"
▾
"This is a change management challenge more than a technical one. The answer is: make the new thing solve a pain they feel today. I don't start by replacing their Excel file. I start by showing them something they can't do in Excel — like a real-time view of promotional performance across all stores, or an automated daily alert when a product is trending below forecast. Once they see value the old tool can't deliver, they migrate themselves. I've seen this pattern play out multiple times — at Microsoft, the biggest adoption wins came when we found the one thing the old system couldn't do, and made the new platform do it beautifully. Pull, not push."
"Can you give an example of when a technical explanation went badly?"
▾
"Early in my career at IBM, I walked into a client meeting with a 20-slide architecture deck — entity-relationship diagrams, data flow arrows, the works. I was proud of it. The CIO sat through about four slides, then said: 'I appreciate the thoroughness, but tell me — will my teams get their reports faster?' I spent 20 minutes on architecture and never answered the only question that mattered. That was the moment I changed my approach. Now I always start with outcomes. The architecture is my backup — it's there if someone asks for it, but it's never my opening act. That lesson has shaped every presentation I've given since."
"How do you handle engineers who think translating for business means you're not technical enough?"
▾
"That's real, and I've experienced it. The way I handle it is by being undeniably technical in the right rooms. When I'm with the engineering team, I'm reviewing their Delta Live Tables pipeline code, discussing partition strategies, and debating merge vs. overwrite patterns. They see that I can go deep. Then when they see me translate the same work into business language for an executive, they actually start to respect that as its own skill — because most of them know they struggle with it. Over time, the best engineers start coming to me before presentations saying 'Can you help me frame this?' That's when you know you've earned both sides."

Closing Line

"The best Solutions Architect isn't the one who knows the most about the technology. It's the one who can make everyone in the room — the VP, the engineer, and the analyst — feel like the technology was built for their specific problem. That's what I do."
