Behavioral Strategic / Change Mgmt

"You're in the first 90 days. Engineering teams have been building pipelines their own way for years. How do you introduce architectural standards without alienating the people who've been keeping the lights on?"

Wegmans Solutions Architect Prep  ·  Steve Lysik  ·  ~3 min answer
Pocket Answer 30–45 sec
"The first thing I would not do is show up with a 50-page standards doc. My approach is three phases. First 30 days — listen and map. Pair with each team, understand why they built things the way they did, find the bright spots already working well. Especially at a company like Wegmans where people have deep institutional knowledge and tenure — that context is invaluable. I'm not coming in to replace what they know; I'm coming in to give them better tools so their expertise scales. Days 30 to 60 — pick one or two shared pain points and solve them with reusable templates. A DLT pipeline pattern, a Unity Catalog naming convention. Make the right way also the easy way. Days 60 to 90 — now I've earned trust, so I form a lightweight architecture working group with respected engineers from each team and we co-author standards together. New work follows the standards; existing pipelines migrate organically via strangler fig. This is exactly how I operated at Microsoft coming into Fortune 500 accounts. The principle: standards people feel ownership over stick — standards imposed by the new architect get resented."
This is the quick version. Scroll down for the full 3-minute phased answer with experience bridges, follow-up Q&A, and coaching notes.

Why They're Asking This

Wegmans has a team of 5 architects and engineering teams with established workflows. They need someone who can raise the bar on consistency and quality without creating friction. This question reveals whether you're a collaborate-first leader or a top-down mandate person. They've probably been burned by both — architects who never ship standards, and architects who shove standards down people's throats on day one.

Scripted Answer — Three Phases
Phase 1 · Days 1–30

Listen First, Earn the Right to Have an Opinion

"The first thing I would not do is show up with a 50-page standards document. I've seen that movie before and it doesn't end well.

My first 30 days are about listening and mapping. I'd sit with each engineering team — not in a formal review setting, but pairing on actual work. 'Show me how you build a pipeline from scratch. Walk me through what happens when something breaks at 2 AM.' That tells me more than any architecture diagram.

What I'm really doing is cataloging patterns — not looking for what's wrong, but looking for what's already working well. Every team has practices that are smart. Maybe one team has a great approach to schema evolution. Another team has solid alerting. I want to find those bright spots because those become the seeds of standards that people actually respect — because they came from their peers, not from the new architect."
1
Shadow each team — pair on real work, ask "show me" not "tell me." Understand their constraints and why they built things the way they did.
2
Catalog the landscape — tools, patterns, pain points, bright spots. Build a mental (or literal) map of the current state without judgment.
3
Name the bright spots publicly — "Team X has a really elegant approach to handling late-arriving data." This builds trust and signals you're not here to tear everything down.
Your Experience Bridge

"At Microsoft, working with ExxonMobil and FedEx, I was the external architect coming into organizations with decades of established data engineering practice. The first thing I learned was that the people running those pipelines knew things about their data that no architecture review could surface. My job was to understand their context first."

Phase 2 · Days 30–60

Prove Value Before Asking for Change

"By day 30, I know where the pain is. And there's always shared pain — pipelines that break in the same way, data quality issues that multiple teams fight independently, onboarding new engineers takes too long because nothing is documented the same way.

So I pick one or two high-visibility pain points and solve them. Not by rewriting someone's pipeline, but by creating something reusable — maybe it's a DLT template with built-in data quality expectations, or a Unity Catalog namespace convention that makes cross-team data discovery actually work, or a CI/CD pattern for Databricks Workflows that eliminates the 'it worked in my notebook' problem.

The key is: I'm not mandating anything yet. I'm saying, 'Hey, I built this thing that solves the problem you told me about. Try it. If it works, great. If not, tell me why and we'll improve it together.' When people adopt something because it makes their life easier rather than because they were told to, it sticks."
1
Solve shared pain first — pick problems that multiple teams complained about. Solving these builds credibility across the org, not just with one team.
2
Ship reusable assets — DLT templates, notebook templates, CI/CD patterns, monitoring dashboards. Make the "right way" also the "easy way."
3
Codify existing best practices — write up the bright spots you found in Phase 1 as the start of your patterns library. Credit the teams who originated them.
Your Experience Bridge

"At IBM, leading the $25M analytics platform, we had 50TB+ of data across teams that had built independently for years. I didn't start with a governance mandate — I started by building shared ingestion patterns that eliminated duplicate work. When teams saw they could onboard a new data source in days instead of weeks, they asked for more standardization."

Phase 3 · Days 60–90

Co-Author Standards, Don't Dictate Them

"Now I have trust and proof points. This is when standards actually land.

I'd form a lightweight architecture working group — not a bureaucratic review board, but a group of respected engineers from each team, people whose opinions carry weight with their peers. We co-author the standards together. My role is to facilitate and synthesize, not dictate.

The standards themselves should be thin and opinionated: a naming convention for Unity Catalog objects, a pipeline template with built-in observability, a data quality contract pattern, a branching and deployment strategy. Things that reduce cognitive load when someone moves between teams or picks up someone else's work.

And critically — standards apply to new work. I'm not asking anyone to rewrite pipelines that are running fine in production. We use a strangler fig approach: as pipelines naturally need updates or new features, they migrate to the new patterns. Over time, the old approaches get replaced organically rather than through a disruptive big-bang migration."
1
Architecture working group — include respected ICs from each team. They become ambassadors, not enforcers. Rotate membership so it doesn't become a bottleneck.
2
Inner-source model — publish standards as a living repo. Anyone can propose changes via PR. Standards evolve with the teams, not apart from them.
3
Strangler fig for legacy — existing pipelines get migrated only when they need changes. No forced rewrites. This respects the investment teams have already made.
4
Lightweight guardrails — Unity Catalog policies, Databricks Asset Bundles for deploy consistency, automated linting. Make compliance automatic, not manual.
Your Experience Bridge

"This mirrors what I'm doing now at Hopewell — coming into client environments as an outside architect. The pattern is always the same: earn trust by listening, prove value by solving pain, then formalize what's already working. The standards that stick are the ones the team feels ownership over."

Anti-Patterns to Avoid Saying

"We need to standardize everything before we ship anything new." — Sounds like a bottleneck, not a leader. They'll hear "nothing gets done until the architect approves it."
"The current state is a mess." — Insults the people who built it. Even if it's true, frame it as "organic evolution that served its purpose."
"At my last company we did X." — Without context about why it fits here, this sounds like you're trying to transplant someone else's org chart.
"We'll do a full audit first." — Engineers hear "inspection" and get defensive. Call it "landscape mapping" or "learning the environment."

Coaching Notes — What Makes This Answer Win

Do This

Lead with empathy — "I would NOT show up with a 50-page standards doc" immediately signals self-awareness
Use the phrase "keeping the lights on" back at them — it shows you heard the question and respect what exists
Mention specific Databricks tools (DLT, Unity Catalog, Asset Bundles) to keep it concrete, not theoretical
Credit teams, not yourself — "they already had a great approach" > "I implemented a new standard"
Reference your actual experience of coming in as an external architect (Microsoft, Hopewell) — this is literally your day job

Avoid This

Don't be too theoretical — they want to hear a specific 90-day plan, not change management philosophy
Don't skip to Phase 3 — if you jump straight to "I'd form a working group," you sound naive about earning trust
Don't say "it depends" without then committing to a concrete approach
Don't focus only on tools — this question is 70% about people, 30% about technology
Don't promise overnight transformation — "this takes 6-12 months to really mature" shows realistic expectations
Likely Follow-Ups
"What if a team just refuses to adopt the standards?" ▼
"First, I'd want to understand why. Usually resistance falls into three buckets: they don't see the value, the standard doesn't fit their use case, or they don't have time to migrate.

If it's a value problem, that's on me — I haven't demonstrated enough benefit. I'd go back to pairing with that team and solving a specific pain point using the standard approach so they see the difference firsthand.

If it's a fit problem, that's good feedback — maybe the standard needs an extension or a documented exception. Not every pipeline is the same.

If it's a time problem, that's a prioritization conversation with leadership, not an architecture conversation. I'd help them make the case for tech debt reduction alongside their feature work.

What I wouldn't do is escalate to management or make it a compliance issue. The moment standards become a stick instead of a tool, you've lost."
"How do you measure whether your standards are actually working?" ▼
"I look at leading indicators, not lagging ones. The lagging indicator is 'percent of pipelines that follow the standard' — that's a vanity metric that incentivizes checkbox compliance.

The leading indicators I care about:
Time-to-production for a new pipeline — if standards include good templates, this should drop.
Incident frequency — standardized observability and quality checks should reduce production issues.
Cross-team PR activity — if engineers from Team A are contributing to Team B's patterns repo, the inner-source model is working.
Onboarding time — can a new engineer understand and contribute to a pipeline they didn't build? That's the ultimate test of whether standards are creating shared understanding."
"Where's the line between helpful standards and bureaucracy?" ▼
"My test is simple: does this standard accelerate teams or slow them down?

If an engineer has to file a ticket and wait for an architecture review to deploy a straightforward pipeline, that's bureaucracy. If they can grab a tested template, plug in their logic, and deploy through an automated pipeline with built-in guardrails — that's a standard doing its job.

I think of standards in two tiers. Hard guardrails are the non-negotiables — data classification in Unity Catalog, PII handling, access control patterns. Those get enforced through automation, not process. Soft guidelines are the conventions — naming, folder structure, documentation patterns. Those are 'you should unless you have a good reason not to, and you document why.'

The moment your standards require more meetings, you've crossed the line."
"How does this work at Wegmans specifically — with SAP pipelines alongside Databricks?" ▼
"That's actually a great example of why listening first matters so much. SAP-sourced pipelines have very different characteristics than web clickstream or IoT data. The extraction patterns, change data capture logic, schema semantics — they're all domain-specific.

I'd want to understand how the SAP integration team is currently operating before suggesting any changes. My approach would be to standardize the Databricks layer — medallion architecture, Unity Catalog governance, data quality expectations — while respecting that the source-specific ingestion layer may legitimately need different patterns for SAP ODP extraction versus Event Hub streaming versus batch file loads.

The standard isn't 'everyone builds pipelines the same way.' It's 'everyone lands data into a consistent lakehouse structure so downstream consumers don't have to care how it got there.' That's where the real value is for a retail organization — analysts and data scientists shouldn't have to know whether a data point came from SAP S/4 HANA or the POS system."

Strong Closing Line

"At the end of the day, my job as an architect isn't to be the smartest person in the room about how pipelines should work. It's to make it easy for every team to build things the right way — and to make sure we collectively agree on what 'the right way' means. That only happens when people feel ownership, not obligation."
