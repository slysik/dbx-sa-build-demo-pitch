Technical Governance Retail / Compliance

"Wegmans handles sensitive customer data — loyalty programs, purchasing history, payment information — plus employee and supplier data. How would you design a data governance framework on Databricks that balances security and compliance with enabling self-service analytics?"

Wegmans Solutions Architect Prep  ·  Steve Lysik  ·  ~4 min answer
Pocket Answer 30–45 sec
"I'd build governance around Unity Catalog as the single control plane with a four-tier data classification system — Restricted for PCI and PII like payment data, Confidential for loyalty purchase history and employee records, Internal for aggregated store metrics, and Public for published reports. Each tier maps to specific Unity Catalog controls: row-level security, column masking on PII fields, and attribute-based access by team role. The key design principle is governance as infrastructure, not process — controls are automated through Unity Catalog policies and DLT quality expectations, not manual reviews. Business teams get self-service SQL Warehouses that operate within pre-defined governance boundaries, so they can explore freely without ever seeing raw PII. Column-level lineage in Unity Catalog means we can trace any metric back to its source for audit. I built this exact pattern at Microsoft for financial services clients where regulatory requirements were even stricter."

Scripted Answer — Four Parts
Part 1 — Frame the Problem: Two Goals, Not One (30s)
"The way I think about governance is that it has two jobs, not one. The obvious job is protection — making sure PCI data, customer PII, and sensitive employee information are locked down. But the less obvious and equally important job is enablement — making sure your store analytics teams, merchandising analysts, and data scientists can actually use the data without filing a ticket every time they need a new column.

If governance only does the first job, you end up with a data platform nobody uses. If it only does the second, you end up on the front page. The architecture has to do both simultaneously."
Part 2 — Data Classification: The Foundation (60s)
"Everything starts with data classification. I'd establish four tiers, and these tiers drive every downstream policy automatically through Unity Catalog tags.

Restricted — PCI cardholder data, SSNs, raw payment transactions. Zero self-service access to raw data. Tokenized or masked views only. This is non-negotiable for PCI-DSS compliance.

Confidential — Loyalty program purchase history, employee HR data, supplier contracts, individual customer profiles. Accessible to authorized roles with column masking on identifiable fields.

Internal — Aggregated store performance, category sales by region, inventory levels, demand forecasts. This is where most analysts live. Broadly accessible within the organization.

Public — Published dashboards, annual metrics, investor-ready data. No restrictions.

In Unity Catalog, these tiers become tags on tables, columns, and schemas. A column tagged pii:restricted automatically gets a masking policy applied — analysts querying that table see **** instead of the raw value. No manual process, no approval queue."

Part 3 — The Protection Layer: How Unity Catalog Enforces It (90s)
"Unity Catalog gives us three enforcement mechanisms that map directly to these tiers.

Column masking — for Confidential and Restricted data. A loyalty analyst querying the customer table sees customer_id and purchase_category but the email column returns a SHA-256 hash and payment_card returns XXXX-XXXX-XXXX-1234. The masking function is defined once in Unity Catalog and enforced everywhere — notebooks, SQL Warehouses, Power BI, AI agents via MCP. There's no way to bypass it because it's applied at the catalog level, not the query level.

Row-level security — a regional merchandising manager sees only data for their stores. A store manager sees only their location. This is critical for Wegmans' organizational structure — you don't want a Rochester store manager browsing Buffalo's supplier contracts. Implemented as dynamic filters in Unity Catalog tied to user attributes.

Attribute-based access control — rather than granting access table-by-table, I'd use role-based grants at the schema level. The wegmans_retail.gold_analytics schema is open to all analysts. The wegmans_retail.pci_restricted schema requires a specific security role plus audit logging on every query. This is scalable governance — you're not managing thousands of individual table permissions."
Your Experience Bridge

"At Microsoft, I designed governance frameworks for financial services clients — ExxonMobil for energy trading data, FedEx for shipment PII. Financial services has even stricter requirements than retail, so the patterns translate directly. The key lesson: governance that requires human approval for every access request doesn't scale. Part 4 — The Enablement Layer: Self-Service Within Guardrails (60s)
"Now the part that makes this actually useful for business teams, not just compliant.

I'd create a curated Gold catalog — wegmans_retail.gold_analytics — that contains pre-built, governed views of the data. Daily sales by store by category. Inventory positions. Demand forecasts. Supplier scorecards. All the masking and row filters are already baked in. A merchandising analyst connects Power BI to this catalog and they're productive in minutes, not days.

For more exploratory work, I'd provide sandbox schemas — wegmans_retail.sandbox_{team} — where analysts can create their own tables, run experiments, join datasets. They still can't see restricted data because masking is enforced at the source, but they have freedom to explore within their classification tier.

And for discoverability, Unity Catalog's data marketplace feature lets teams publish curated datasets with descriptions, sample queries, and usage guidelines. Instead of asking 'who owns this table?' in Slack, they search the catalog. Self-service doesn't mean ungoverned. It means governed access that doesn't require a ticket."

Likely Follow-Ups
"How do you handle PCI-DSS specifically? Payment data has strict requirements." ▼
"PCI-DSS is about minimizing the cardholder data environment. My approach: payment data never lands in the lakehouse in its raw form. The POS system tokenizes at the point of capture — what lands in Bronze is already tokenized. The token-to-card mapping lives in a separate PCI-scoped vault outside Databricks entirely.

In the lakehouse, the pci_restricted schema contains only tokenized transaction records. Analysts can do full sales analysis on amounts, timestamps, product associations — everything they need — without ever seeing a card number. If a fraud investigation requires de-tokenization, that's a separate, audited process through the vault, not through Databricks.

This architecture keeps the Databricks environment out of PCI scope entirely, which dramatically simplifies compliance. The platform processes transactions, not cardholder data."
"What about the loyalty program data? Customers share personal info — how do you balance analytics with privacy?" ▼
"Loyalty data is the most valuable and sensitive intersection. Customers share their shopping patterns, dietary preferences, email, phone — that's Confidential tier.

The architecture creates two views of the same data. The analytics view in Gold strips PII — you see loyalty_segment, purchase_frequency, preferred_categories, but not name or email. This is what merchandising teams use for category planning and promotional targeting.

The marketing view retains identifiable fields but is restricted to the CRM team with explicit access grants and full audit logging. They need the email for campaign delivery, but they access it through a governed view, not raw tables.

Unity Catalog's column masking makes this seamless — same underlying table, different projections based on the querying user's role. No data duplication, no sync issues."
"How do you get business teams to actually adopt the governed platform instead of building shadow IT?" ▼
"Shadow IT happens when the governed path is slower than the ungoverned path. My approach is to make the governed path the fastest path.

That means: curated Gold datasets that answer 80% of questions out of the box. SQL Warehouse access that connects to Power BI in one click. Sandbox schemas where analysts can experiment without filing tickets. Data marketplace so they can discover what's available.

The first 90 days, I'd identify what analysts are currently pulling from spreadsheets, manual exports, or direct database queries — and make sure those use cases are the first things available in the governed catalog. If the platform doesn't serve their immediate needs, no amount of governance policy will prevent workarounds."
"How does this governance model work with AI agents and MCP Servers?" ▼
"This is where Unity Catalog really shines. MCP Servers in Databricks query through Unity Catalog, which means every AI agent inherits the same governance framework as a human analyst. An AI agent answering 'what were our top-selling categories last week?' queries the Gold catalog and gets the same masked, row-filtered view that a human analyst would.

The agent's service principal in Unity Catalog has specific grants — it can access gold_analytics but not pci_restricted. Every query is audit-logged. Model lineage tracks which data trained which model.

This is the future of data governance — it's not just about human access anymore, it's about governing AI access with the same rigor. The classification tiers and Unity Catalog controls I described work identically for AI agents. That's by design."

Coaching Notes

Do This

Lead with "two jobs, not one" — immediately signals you won't over-index on security at the expense of usability
Name Wegmans-specific data types (loyalty, POS, supplier contracts) — shows you've thought about their world
Emphasize "governance as infrastructure, not process" — this is the line they'll remember
Connect MCP Servers to governance — ties back to their JD and shows forward thinking
Reference financial services experience — regulated industries carry credibility for data protection

Avoid This

Don't recite PCI-DSS requirements like a checklist — they have compliance people for that. You're the architect.
Don't say "we'd lock everything down" — that's the opposite of what they want to hear
Don't ignore the self-service side — if 80% of your answer is about protection, you've missed the point
Don't get lost in Unity Catalog syntax — concepts over SQL statements
Don't forget employee and supplier data — the question mentions it, so address it (Confidential tier)

Strong Closing Line

"The best governance framework is one that analysts don't even notice. They connect to the catalog, they find the data they need, they build their analysis — and behind the scenes, every access is classified, masked appropriately, logged, and traceable. The security team sleeps well, the analytics team is productive, and the architecture handles the tension between those two goals automatically. That's what Unity Catalog enables when it's designed correctly."
